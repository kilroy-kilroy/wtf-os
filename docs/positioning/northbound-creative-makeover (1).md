# The Makeover: Northbound Creative

A worked example of what comes out the other end of the self-serve audit. Fictional agency, real mechanics. This is what you would show a prospect to prove the thing actually does something.

---

## THE BEFORE

**Northbound Creative.** Roughly $4M, 22 people, Portland. Branding, web, a little paid. The work is genuinely good. Nobody outside their referral network has any idea they exist. Classic: quietly excellent, loudly anonymous.

**Their current homepage hero:**

**Northbound Creative** A full-service creative agency building brands that resonate. Results-driven. Strategic. Your partner in growth. \[Let's Talk\]

**Charlie Brown Detector score: 81% wah-wah.**

Flagged phrases: "full-service," "brands that resonate," "results-driven," "strategic," "your partner in growth." Every one of them is a sound the adults make in a Peanuts cartoon. The site bounces off everyone equally, which feels safe and is actually fatal. A founder in crisis reads that and feels nothing, so they scroll, so they leave.

---

## THE INTERVIEW (abridged)

Robot-Tim talking to Dana Reyes, founder. The transformation happens in the push-backs, so those are what is shown.

**Node 0 \- Where are you actually going**

**Robot-Tim:** Not the pitch-deck version. The real one. Where are you going? **Dana:** I want to get to like fifteen million and build a real leadership layer. **Robot-Tim:** Why fifteen? What does fifteen buy you that four does not? **Dana:** Honestly? I want to stop being the only one who can land the big clients. I want the work to be the magnet, not me. **Robot-Tim:** Good. That is the real answer, and it changes everything. Right now you are the positioning. We have to move it into the brand so it stops living in your calendar.

**Node 1 \- Favorite clients**

**Dana:** A skincare founder mid-pivot, a B2B software guy after his raise, a family food company handing off to the second generation. **Robot-Tim:** What is the thread? Say it fast, do not think. **Dana:** They were all... in the middle of becoming something. Scared. The company had changed and they had not caught up to it yet. **Robot-Tim:** Write that down word for word. That is not a client type. That is a moment. You sell to a moment.

**Node 3 \- The thing they did not buy** (the keystone)

**Robot-Tim:** What did they get from you that was never on the invoice? **Dana:** A better website and a cleaner brand system. **Robot-Tim:** No. That is what they paid for. What did they get that surprised them? **Dana:** ...One of them cried in the final presentation. She said we made her sound like the company she always thought she was building but could never explain. **Robot-Tim:** There it is. You do not sell websites. You give founders the words for the thing they already are. The website is just where the words live.

**Node 5 \- Your enemies**

**Robot-Tim:** What makes you say what the f when you see it done? **Dana:** Agencies that treat brand like decoration. New logo, new colors, same confused company underneath. **Robot-Tim:** Now you have an enemy. And the rule: the enemy is the decoration mindset, never the founders who fell for it, because it looked like the responsible thing to buy.

**Node 6 \- What you are like**

**Dana:** Warm but direct. We will tell you the thing your team is too polite to say. We swear a little. **Robot-Tim:** So you would walk into American Express in good jeans and not apologize. **Dana:** Exactly that. **Robot-Tim:** Then the website cannot wear a tie. The whole trip has to feel like you.

## THE AFTER: Narrative Spine Starter

**The one-liner (VVV):** We give founder-led companies the words for the thing they already are.

**Who this is for:** Founder-led companies, roughly $2M to $30M, hitting an inflection (a raise, a pivot, a generational handoff, a growth spurt) where the story the market hears is about three years behind the company they have actually become.

**Who this is NOT for (said out loud, on purpose):** Committee-run brands shopping for a vendor to execute a pre-baked brief. Anyone who wants a logo and a palette and calls that a brand. Anyone whose brief includes the words "make it pop."

**The problem they think they have:** "Our brand looks dated and inconsistent. We need a refresh."

**The problem they actually have:** The company evolved and the story stayed put. The market is still buying the old version of them. A coat of paint cannot fix a meaning problem, and every month the gap gets more expensive.

**The value they did not buy:** They came for a website. They left able to say what they stand for in a single sentence, and brave enough to say it in public.

**The three traps** (each framed as a trap smart people fall into):

1. **The Refresh Trap.** Treating a meaning problem like a paint problem. New logo, same confused company.  
2. **The Consensus Trap.** Letting the whole exec team vote the edges off the story until it is smooth, safe, and says absolutely nothing. That is how you end up sounding like everyone.  
3. **The More-Words Trap.** Believing that if you just explain harder, people will finally get it. The fix is almost always saying less, truer.

**Three "am I in the right place" headlines, in Dana's real voice:**

1. You know what you have built. You just cannot say it out loud yet.  
2. For founder-led companies whose story is three years behind the company they became.  
3. Your brand is not broken. It outgrew its own words.

## THE VISIBLE MAKEOVER

**Before:**

A full-service creative agency building brands that resonate. Results-driven. Strategic. Your partner in growth.

**After:**

**You know what you have built. You just cannot say it out loud yet.** Northbound helps founder-led companies catch their story up to the company they actually became. Not a refresh. The words. \[Show me the gap\]

Same agency. Same people. Same good work. The only thing that changed is they stopped describing the serving dish and started naming the meal. A scared founder mid-pivot reads the after version and feels seen in about four seconds, which is the entire job of a homepage.

That is the makeover. The robot got them this far. The human installs it everywhere.
